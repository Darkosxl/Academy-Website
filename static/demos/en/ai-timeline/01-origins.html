The First Neurons

1943 – 1958 · three ideas that started everything

1943 · a math neuron 1955 · "AI" gets its name 1958 · it learns!
In 1943, two researchers wrote a brain cell down as plain math: add the inputs, fire if the sum is big enough. By 1958 there was a machine that could adjust itself — it learned to sort dots with nobody tuning it. Then someone drew four dots that no straight line can split, and that one picture froze the whole field for decades. On this page you get to run all three moments yourself.

1943 — a brain cell, as math

warm-up · side quest

Warren McCulloch & Walter Pitts sketch the first mathematical model of a neuron.

You're about to run a 1943 "math brain cell" by hand and find out exactly when it decides to fire.

try this
  1. click the blue inputs on and off → watch Σ climb and the lamp light the moment Σ ≥ θ
  2. drag the θ slider → watch the same inputs suddenly stop being "enough"
  3. hit the ⚡ AND / OR chips → find the θ that builds each logic gate
y = 1 if Σi xi ≥ θ , else y = 0

decoder: the lamp = the neuron firing (y = 1) · θ = the bar the input-sum has to clear.

What you just saw is called a threshold neuron: sum + threshold, nothing more. Its three limits — inputs only 0 or 1, every input counts exactly the same, and θ picked by hand for every job — are exactly why 1958 mattered.

1955 — the field gets a name

interlude · quick flip-through

The 1956 summer workshop whose 1955 proposal named the field — the first document ever to use the words "artificial intelligence".

A Proposal for the
Dartmouth Summer Research Project
on Artificial Intelligence

J. McCarthy · M. L. Minsky · N. Rochester · C. E. Shannon
August 31, 1955

We propose that a 2 month, 10 man study of artificial intelligence be carried out during the summer of 1956 at Dartmouth College... The study is to proceed on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it.

names the field: "ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE"

1958 — the Perceptron learns by itself

★ the main event

Frank Rosenblatt builds the first neural network. Three upgrades over 1943: inputs can be any number, every input gets its own weight wi… and the weights change on their own.

y = 1 if Σi wixi ≥ 0 , else y = 0

Where did θ go? The bias trick: add a fake input x₀ = 1 with weight w₀ = −θ. The hand-picked threshold just became another weight — so it gets learned automatically too.

You're about to watch a machine fix its own mistakes — no human touches the knobs.

The line is where Σwixi = 0. Points with a red ring are mistakes. Click the plot to add your own points and make its job harder.

decoder: the line = the machine's current rule for telling the two groups apart — everything on one side gets called ●, the other side ▲.

What you just saw is called the perceptron learning rule: every mistake nudges the weights a little, and the nudges pile up until the line splits the two groups — no human tuning needed.
