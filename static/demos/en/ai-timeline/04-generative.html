Machines That Dream

1985 → 2014 · how computers learned to make things up — not just recognize them
1985: two scientists borrow a trick from physics and build machines that dream — by rolling a ball downhill. 2014: a young researcher gets into an argument in a Montreal bar, goes home, and by morning has invented machines that forge reality. This page walks that road: two quick warm-ups, then the main event — a forgery game where you play the detective, and finally the same duel fought machine-vs-machine.

① a ball, a landscape

warm-up #1

Before any forging — how does a machine imagine anything at all? The 1985 trick: turn "making things up" into rolling downhill.

decoder: valleys = things that look real, hills = things that look fake, and the ball = the machine dreaming up one sample.

try this
  1. tap anywhere on the hills to drop the ball → watch it roll down and settle in a valley
  2. press "poke the ball" → wherever it lands, it finds its way to something real-looking
  3. drag temperature to hot → watch the ball get too jumpy to commit, hopping between valleys
the ball is rolling…
what you just saw This is the Boltzmann Machine idea — 1985, Geoffrey Hinton & Terrence Sejnowski, borrowed straight from statistical physics: give real data LOW energy (valleys) and fakes HIGH energy (hills), then generate by letting it roll downhill with a little thermal shaking. That landing is the machine generating a sample.

② the machine itself

warm-up #2

Here is the whole 1985 machine doing that downhill roll: 7 units, every one wired to every other, flipping at random and slowly cooling until they agree.

decoder: each circle = one on/off switch, and the whole flickering pattern = the ball from ① — settling = landing in a valley.

press it → watch the temperature fall and the pattern lock into a different "memory"
what you just saw A real Boltzmann Machine (named after the physicist) settling into a low-energy state — and nobody told the hidden blue units what to look for: the white units v₁…v₄ touch the data, the blue h₁…h₃ never do, yet they discover features on their own. Grandchildren of this idea: VAEs and today's diffusion image generators.

③ spot the fakes — a game

★ the main event

2014's big idea — a GAN — is two networks playing a game: a forger and a detective. You're about to BE the detective, against a forger that improves every round.

decoder: pretend the smooth stripes are real photos and the blocky mess is a bad forgery.

try this
  1. round 1: tap the pictures you think are fake, then "check my answers" → easy, the forger is brand new
  2. round 2: the forger has trained in between → look much closer before you tap
  3. round 3: watch your score fall toward 50% → that's a coin flip

8 of these 16 pictures follow the true rule (smooth stripes). 8 are forgeries. Tap the ones you think are fake, then check.

You just played the discriminator. In every round the fakes came from a generator — and each round it got better at imitating the real rule, so your accuracy slid toward 50%… a coin flip. When the best detector is reduced to guessing, the generator has won — its fakes are, for all practical purposes, real.
what you just saw You were the DISCRIMINATOR. When the best detective is reduced to guessing, the forger — the GENERATOR — has won: that is exactly the moment a GAN is done training.

④ the duel, automated

the closer

The game you just played — but now both sides are machines, playing thousands of rounds per second.

decoder: blue bumps = the real data · orange = the forger's fakes (starting way off) · gray dashes = what the detector believes. THE number to watch: detector accuracy, 95% → 50%.

what you just saw That was a GAN — a Generative Adversarial Network (Ian Goodfellow, 2014): a generator forges data, a discriminator calls real-or-fake, and they train against each other — the forger improves to fool the detector, the detector improves to catch it — until the orange sits exactly on the blue and the detector is flipping coins.
Famous origin story: Goodfellow dreamed it up after an argument in a Montreal bar — and coded the first working version that same night.
GANs powered the first wave of hyper-realistic image synthesis — the "this person does not exist" faces and the first deepfakes were GAN babies.
Yann LeCun called adversarial training "the most interesting idea in machine learning in the last twenty years."
